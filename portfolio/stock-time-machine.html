The concept

Every portfolio looks obvious in hindsight.

I built this project to explore time-series data handling and dynamic charting in the Power Platform environment — using real historical stock price paths. It answers the classic "what if" question with real mathematical precision, applying splits and reinvestments to show what a small decision in the past would be worth in 2026. While it's less corporate than the Regulation Inspector or the AI Risk Assessment Workflow, it runs on the exact same principles of data structuring and interactive dashboarding.

How it works

The time-series pipeline

01

Select & travel

Pick the asset and the starting year — the engine immediately pulls the historical base price from that specific date.

02

Apply math

The system calculates shares purchased (Investment / Price) and adjusts for historic stock splits along the timeline.

03

Generate trajectory

A dynamic path is compiled matching the actual monthly trajectory of the asset from the start year to 2026.

04

Deliver verdict

The results surface instantly — both as high-level returns and a custom, slightly sarcastic narrative explaining what you missed out on.

Reflection

What building this sharpened

Time-series data modelling

Handling dates, stock splits, and price sequences requires a highly structured table schema — not just flat arrays. This project forced clean data engineering habits before any charts were drawn.

Dynamic SVG generation

Building working visualisations without bloated libraries means coding raw SVGs directly from the dataset. It's a useful skill when you need high-performance charts in light dashboards.

User engagement comes from micro-copy

A simple gain/loss calculator is dry. Adding a slightly tongue-in-cheek "verdict" box (explaining that your $1,000 NVDA buy would have paid off your mortgage) is what makes people play with a dashboard.

Design for responsive rendering

Charts that look good on desktop often fail on mobile screens. The dynamic SVG viewports were designed with fluid proportions so the stock trajectory remains readable at any aspect ratio.
