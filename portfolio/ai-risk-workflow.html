The problem

AI systems get deployed faster than they get assessed.

In most organisations, AI risk assessment lives in Word documents, email chains, and untracked spreadsheet approvals. The result is predictable: inconsistent risk scoring between teams, slow approval cycles, no visibility for leadership, and no single source of truth when a regulator (or an internal auditor) asks which AI systems are running and who signed them off. This concept explores whether the same governance discipline applied to regulatory text in Regulation Inspector can be applied to the AI systems themselves, using the Microsoft Power Platform as the delivery vehicle.

The approach

From ad-hoc documents to a structured workflow

01

Standardised intake

A guided Power Apps form captures every use case the same way (owner, model type, dataset sources, intended use), with conditional privacy questions when personal data is involved.

02

Automated scoring

Power Automate scores each submission against weighted risk factors: privacy, bias, security, transparency, producing a consistent overall rating instead of a gut feel.

03

Routed approvals

Risk level drives routing: high-risk cases go to Privacy, Security, and AI Governance; low-risk cases take a lighter path. SLA reminders and escalations keep approvals moving.

04

Live reporting

Every decision lands in Dataverse and surfaces in Power BI — risk distribution, cycle times, bottlenecks, and a full model inventory by business unit.

Process flow

From form to dashboard

How the components hand off to each other: Power Apps captures the submission, Dataverse holds the record (with evidence files in SharePoint), Power Automate orchestrates scoring and approvals through Teams and Outlook, and every decision lands back in the same dataset that Power BI reports from.

01Form submission creates the assessment record 02Evidence files land in SharePoint — references linked in Dataverse 03Flow triggers on the new record and calculates the risk score 04Approval requests go out via Teams cards and Outlook — decisions return to the flow 05Scores, approvals, and the audit log are written back 06Power BI reads the live dataset — no exports, no copies

Storage is a swappable layer: Dataverse is the system of record in this design, with SharePoint holding evidence files. The same pattern runs on SharePoint Lists for lighter-weight tenants without premium licensing, or on Azure SQL where scale and existing reporting infrastructure justify it. The flow logic and dashboards don't change; only the connector does.

Workflow logic

The flow does the chasing, so people don't have to.

Approvals route by risk level. Automated reminders and escalation kick in when an SLA is breached, so nothing needs chasing by email.

⚑

Finalise & log

A risk summary is generated, the audit log is stored, and the dashboards update, all from the same event.

CONDITION — HIGH RISK?
Yes → route to all three stakeholders: Privacy → Security → AI Governance.  No → route to Privacy only. Routing runs parallel or sequential depending on risk level, so low-risk use cases aren't stuck in a queue built for high-risk ones.

Reflection

Four things worth remembering

Centralise logic in the data layer

Keeping scoring rules and risk factors in Dataverse, not hard-coded into individual flows, means one change propagates everywhere. The same lesson that shaped Regulation Inspector's config-driven taxonomy applies here.

Approval paths are a people problem first

The hardest part isn't the flow. It's getting Privacy, Security, and AI Governance to agree on who decides what, in which order. Stakeholder workshops belong at the start of the build, not the end.

Risk scoring is never finished

AI systems evolve after deployment, so a scoring model that's fixed at intake goes stale. The design assumes periodic reassessment as a first-class part of the workflow, not an exception.

Dashboards drive engagement

Leadership doesn't read assessment records. They look at the risk distribution chart. Making governance visible is what turns it from a compliance chore into something the business actually steers by.
