The problem

Compliance teams read regulation once and then lose track of it in spreadsheets.

Regulatory documents like DORA span hundreds of articles across wildly different operational domains — ICT risk, business continuity, incident reporting, third-party oversight. Reviewing them manually is slow, and once a gap is identified, tracking its remediation tends to live in disconnected trackers that fall out of date. I built this project to see whether that first pass — reading, classifying, and structuring a regulation — could be automated well enough to hand a compliance team a live dashboard instead of a static PDF annotation.

The approach

From PDF to structured, trackable data

01

Upload & extract

Parse the regulation PDF while preserving article and paragraph structure — not just raw text.

02

Classify

Each clause is tagged by functional domain and marked mandatory or discretionary based on its language.

03

Review

A human confirms or overrides the AI's tag, then decides: covered by an internal document, a genuine gap, or not applicable. Nothing counts as a finding until this step happens.

04

Track

Reviewed clauses get an owner and a due date where needed — visualised across three dashboard views, with AI-suggested and human-confirmed always kept visually distinct.

Governance

A "Covered" status is only as good as the link behind it.

Pointing a requirement at an internal document is the easy part. The document moves, gets renamed, gets archived, or the SharePoint folder gets restructured — and if nothing catches that, a clause stays marked "Covered" against evidence that no longer exists. That's a silent compliance gap, which is worse than an open one because nobody's looking for it.

✓

Periodic link check

Every internal reference is checked on a schedule to confirm the linked document still resolves.

→
!

Change detected

If a link breaks, moves, or is renamed, the clause is flagged — its "Covered" status stays visible, but marked for attention rather than silently trusted.

→
⚑

Ticket auto-raised

A ticket routes to the department that owns the domain — not a generic queue — so the right team re-confirms coverage or supplies a new reference.

Try it: in the Tracker view above, Art. 28(4) shows a small warning dot next to its Gap badge — click it to see what a live link-health alert and its auto-raised ticket look like.

Reflection

What this project sharpened

Data modelling over one-off scripts

The domain taxonomy had to live in config, not code, so the same pipeline works for any regulation, not just DORA. That constraint shaped every other decision in the build.

Dashboards are a translation problem

The same clause-level data needs to answer very different questions depending on the audience — a status check, a working list, or a prioritisation view — which is what drove the three-dashboard structure.
